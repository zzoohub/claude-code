# Pricing Research Methods

Methods for determining optimal pricing through customer research.

---

## Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter

Four questions that identify acceptable price range:

1. **Too cheap** — At what price would you question quality?
2. **A bargain** — At what price would it be a great deal?
3. **Getting expensive** — At what price would you hesitate but consider?
4. **Too expensive** — At what price would you never buy?

### Analysis
Plot cumulative distribution of each answer. Key intersections:
- **Point of Marginal Cheapness** — Where "too cheap" crosses "expensive"
- **Point of Marginal Expensiveness** — Where "too expensive" crosses "bargain"
- **Optimal Price Point** — Where "too cheap" crosses "too expensive"
- **Acceptable Range** — Between marginal cheapness and marginal expensiveness

### Best Practices
- Sample size: 100+ respondents per segment
- Segment by persona/company size
- Ask about a specific product configuration
- Combine with MaxDiff for feature packaging

## MaxDiff Analysis

Identifies which features customers value most for tier packaging.

### How It Works
1. Show sets of 4-5 features
2. Ask: "Most important? Least important?"
3. Repeat with different feature combinations
4. Calculate relative importance scores

### Application
- Use results to decide what goes in each tier
- High-value features in premium tiers
- Low-value features can be cut or included everywhere
- Medium-value features differentiate mid-tier

## Conjoint Analysis

Advanced method for understanding feature-price tradeoffs.

### Setup
1. Define attributes (features, price points, support levels)
2. Create product profiles (combinations of attributes)
3. Ask respondents to rank or choose preferred profiles
4. Statistical analysis reveals willingness-to-pay per feature

### When to Use
- Complex products with many features
- Need to understand feature-price tradeoffs
- Larger companies with research budget
- Before major pricing restructure

## Quick Research Methods

### Customer Interviews (10-15 interviews)
- "What would you expect to pay for this?"
- "What alternatives have you considered?"
- "What would make this worth 2x the price?"
- "At what price would you not consider this?"

### Win/Loss Analysis
- Track why deals were won or lost
- If "too expensive" is rare → prices may be too low
- If "too expensive" is common → test lower prices or better value communication

### Competitor Pricing Audit
- Document all competitor pricing (tiers, features, limits)
- Calculate price-per-feature ratios
- Identify positioning gaps
- Update quarterly

## Value-Based Pricing Research

### The 10x Rule
Your product should deliver at least 10x the value of its price. If you charge $100/month, customers should get $1,000+ in value.

### Measuring Value
1. **Time saved** — Hours saved × hourly rate
2. **Revenue generated** — Additional revenue attributable to product
3. **Cost avoided** — Expenses eliminated by using product
4. **Risk reduced** — Cost of problems prevented

### Willingness-to-Pay Ladder
1. What's the maximum you'd pay? (ceiling)
2. What seems fair? (target)
3. At what price is it an obvious yes? (floor)

## Running a Price Test

### What to Test
- Price points (not usually A/B tested live)
- Annual vs. monthly presentation
- Tier naming and positioning
- Feature packaging
- Discount strategies

### Pricing Page A/B Tests (Safe)
- Layout and presentation
- Tier highlighting
- Annual/monthly toggle default
- Trust signals and social proof

### Price Point Tests (Careful)
- Test with new customer segments only
- Geographic testing (different markets)
- Grandfathering existing customers
- Time-boxed with clear measurement
